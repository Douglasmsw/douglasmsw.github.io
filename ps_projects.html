Lectures on Scientific Modeling and Causality

I had the opportunity to be a paid lecturer for UChicago's undergraduate course Sense & Sensibility & Science, which seeks to distill principles and techniques for decision-making from the scientific disciplines. Put another way, what can we learn from the sciences about how best to validate and weigh information when deliberating? I gave two lectures on scientific modeling and causality.

My first lecture covered what scientific modeling is, its role in the scientific process, how it connects to theory, and how to use formal modeling tools to better reason about claims. My second lecture gives an introduction to the potential outcomes framework and the fundamental problem of causal inference, explores when causality is relevant in science, and connects these concepts to formal scientific modeling to equip students with more tools to reason about claims.

Essays on Social Scientific Productivity

I am currently developing a series of essays exploring how social science has, is failing to, and can be productive for society. In a political era defined by a lack of institutional trust and growing inequality, the dream of a fulfilling society guided by rigorous social scientific findings feels like more like a fairy tale dreamed up in an ivory tower. Pulling from philosophy of science, the history of the Chicago School of economics, and other works, I want to explore the role academia plays in societal change and think critically about how to go about the practice of social science to tangibly improve lives.
